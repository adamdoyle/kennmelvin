Cranker Release Video

The release is very hard to study because it happens so fast. This clips illustrates how computerised video is a valuable tool to both the modern bowler and the coach. You can slow the clip down, use the mouse to drag the sliding control backwards and forwards or just play the clip over and over to imprint the release in your memory.

This video clip will take a few minutes to load: when it does you'll see the cranker using more wrist action to generate maximum revolutions. Compare this with the stroker or spinner style releases or watch the ball flare-up all the way down the lane. There are links to other video clips at the bottom of this page.

What to look for...

  1. The plant-and-pull style (see the inset picture): the sliding foot is put down with little or no slide to create the leverage needed for this powerful release.
  2. The trailing leg will clear to the left allowing the hand to come through for the release.
  3. The wrist is cupped, cocked and the elbow bent: all this enables the hand to stay behind and under the ball - which means the fingers have the potential to generate maximum lift: this release has 500rpm compared to the stroker at about 300rpm.
  4. At the moment of release the elbow flattens as the wrist uncocks and the fingers drive "through" the ball. Some call it hitting the ball, the urethane or frisbee release, but unless the wrist goes from maximum (cupped) to minimum (broken) maximum revolutions can't be achieved: this is why a wrist support will restrict a cranker. To get past 300rpm you need to remove the wrist support and develop some wrist action.
