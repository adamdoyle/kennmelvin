The Timing in Slow Motion

If you don't start right, you can't finish right, so Don Johnson used to say. Wise words, indeed. I've isolated the the start, the "power step" and the finish in this video clip and used slow motion so you can see exactly what's going on in a well timed approach. You'll see Tomas Leandersson, the winner of the AMF World Cup 2000, demonstrate excellent timing throughout the approach.

What to Look for

  • Notice how, in the start, as the first step is taken, the weight is over the leading foot. This pattern will continue through to the Finish, the only point where the weight gets behind the steps.
  • As the bowler takes the first step look how his bowling arm is fully extended and the ball is going down. Many bowlers hold on to the ball to long in the start, leading to late timing.
  • As the bowler takes the second step, the ball is past the bottom of the swing and is on it's way up into the backswing. This area is what Don Johnson called the "pro-zone" - because so many of the professional bowlers had this timing.
  • The penultimate step, before the slide, is called the pivot or power step: it is a smaller step allowing the bowler to maintain a powerful, balanced position, ready to push off into the slide.
  • in the finish the bowler is using a strong wrist position and extending through the shot to create strong revolutions. Use of the opposite arm is also evident, with the arm being out in front of the body when the backswing is at it's peak and then moving behind the body in the finish at the moment of release.
